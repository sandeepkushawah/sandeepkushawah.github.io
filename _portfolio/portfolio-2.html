Overview

As a glass-forming liquid is cooled toward the glass transition temperature, its relaxation dynamics slow by many orders of magnitude. This slowing is spatially non-uniform: some particles diffuse freely while adjacent particles remain nearly frozen over the same time window. This project investigates how these mobile and immobile particles organize spatially into clusters and string-like cooperative structures, and how the size and lifetime of these structures grow as the glass transition is approached.

Research Questions

  • How do we robustly identify mobile and immobile subpopulations from displacement distributions in an MD trajectory?
  • Do mobile particles cluster spatially beyond what random chance would predict — and how does cluster size scale with temperature?
  • Do mobile particles form string-like cooperative chains, and what is the geometry and length distribution of these strings?
  • How does the characteristic length scale of dynamic heterogeneity relate to the alpha-relaxation time and the approach to the glass transition?

Tools & Methods

Molecular Dynamics simulations of the Kob-Andersen (KA) binary Lennard-Jones mixture across a range of temperatures from the normal liquid into the deeply supercooled regime; particle displacement statistics; cluster-size analysis; string-length distributions.

Status

Ongoing research — results will be presented at the Open Seminar 2 (July 2026) and form part of the PhD thesis (August 2026).
