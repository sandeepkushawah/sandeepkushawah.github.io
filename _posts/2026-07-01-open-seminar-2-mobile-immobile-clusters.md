My second Open Seminar will be held in **July 2026**, focusing on dynamic heterogeneity in supercooled glass-forming liquids — specifically the spatial and temporal organization of mobile and immobile particles into clusters and string-like cooperative structures.

## About the Talk

As a glass-forming liquid is cooled toward the glass transition, its dynamics slow down dramatically but not uniformly. Some particles remain nearly frozen while their neighbours diffuse freely — a phenomenon known as **dynamic heterogeneity**. This seminar will cover:

- **Mobile and immobile particle identification**: How we define and track fast-moving and slow-moving subsets of particles over an observation time window using displacement statistics.
- **Cluster formation**: How mobile (and immobile) particles do not distribute randomly — they aggregate into spatially correlated clusters whose size grows as temperature decreases.
- **String-like cooperative motion**: Mobile particles tend to form one-dimensional string-like chains, suggesting that motion is highly cooperative and correlated at the nanoscale.
- **Connection to the glass transition**: How the growth of these dynamic structures relates to the dramatic slowing of relaxation and the approach to the glass transition temperature.

## Why This Matters

Dynamic heterogeneity is widely regarded as a key fingerprint of the glass transition. Understanding how cooperative motion organizes into clusters and strings gives us insight into the microscopic mechanism behind the enormous viscosity increase observed in glass-forming systems — and may eventually point toward a unified theoretical picture of the glass transition.

This seminar draws on ongoing research from the Soft Condensed Matter group at IIT Mandi using Molecular Dynamics simulations of the Kob-Andersen binary Lennard-Jones mixture.
